## Overview

Huayan Temple (华严寺) is the largest and best-preserved temple complex from the Liao Dynasty (907–1125 AD) in all of China. Built by the Khitan empire when Datong was its western capital, the temple faces east — toward the rising sun, as the Khitan people worshipped — unlike every other Chinese temple which faces south.

The complex is split into two sections: the **Upper Temple** (上寺), home to China's largest Buddhist hall, and the **Lower Temple** (下寺), which houses a 1,000-year-old sutra library that survived wars, earthquakes, and dynastic collapse.

<InfoBox type="highlight">
Huayan Temple is not a tourist stop — it's an active monastery. You'll see monks, hear chanting, and smell incense. This is living Buddhism inside a Liao Dynasty time capsule.
</InfoBox>

## What to See

### The Upper Temple — Mahavira Hall (大雄宝殿)

This is the **largest Buddhist hall in China** — 54m wide, 9 bays across. Built in 1062, rebuilt in 1140 after a fire, it's a rare surviving example of Liao-Jin monumental architecture. Inside: five massive Ming Dynasty Buddhas (each over 5m tall), flanked by 20 heavenly guardians. The ceiling frescoes cover 880 sqm — Ming Dynasty murals that most visitors walk right past without looking up.

### The Lower Temple — Bhagavan Sutra Hall (薄伽教藏殿)

Built in 1038, this is the **oldest wooden structure in Datong**. It was built as a library to store the Buddhist canon, and the interior is lined with 38 intricately carved wooden cabinets (壁藏) — floor-to-ceiling sutra shelves that double as architectural sculpture. These are the finest surviving examples of Liao Dynasty woodwork anywhere.

Inside the hall, look for the famous **"Bodhisattva with Clasped Hands"** (合掌露齿菩萨) — a life-sized clay figure of a bodhisattva smiling with teeth slightly visible. It's so lifelike that locals call her the "Mona Lisa of the East."

### The Pagoda & Gardens

The 43.5m wooden pagoda at the rear of the complex was rebuilt in 2011, but the surrounding gardens are a quiet place to sit and absorb the atmosphere. From the top level, you get a view across the temple rooftops and the ancient city beyond.

<InfoBox type="tip" title="Pro Tip">
Visit in the late afternoon when the low sun hits the Upper Temple hall. The wooden brackets glow golden, and the courtyard is at its most photogenic. Then walk 5 minutes to the Drum Tower area for dinner.
</InfoBox>

## Why It Matters

The Liao Dynasty is one of history's forgotten empires. The Khitan people ruled a territory stretching from Manchuria to Central Asia, yet almost nothing of their civilization survives — except Huayan Temple. Every beam, bracket, and Buddha here was built by a people the world has largely forgotten.
