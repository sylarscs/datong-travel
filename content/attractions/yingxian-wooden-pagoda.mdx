## Overview

The Yingxian Wooden Pagoda (应县木塔), officially the Sakyamuni Pagoda of Fogong Temple, is the **oldest and tallest wooden pagoda in the world**. Built in 1056 AD during the Liao Dynasty, it stands 67.31 meters tall — roughly a 20-story building — constructed entirely from wood, without a single nail.

It has survived **over 40 major earthquakes**, several wars, and 1,000 years of weather. It leans about 2 degrees — more than the Leaning Tower of Pisa did before its stabilization — yet it still stands. Engineers call it a miracle of pre-modern structural design.

<InfoBox type="highlight">
Yingxian is about 75km south of Datong (roughly 1.5 hours by car). It's an easy day trip that most tourists skip — which is exactly why you should go.
</InfoBox>

## What to See

### The Engineering — 54 Bracket Types

The pagoda uses 54 different types of **dougong** (斗拱) — interlocking wooden brackets — to distribute weight and absorb seismic energy. This is the architectural equivalent of a tuned mass damper, predating its "invention" by 900 years. Each level has a different bracket pattern; no two floors are structurally identical.

### The Buddha Statues

Each of the pagoda's five visible floors (there are actually nine levels including hidden structural floors) houses a Buddha:
- **Ground floor**: An 11-meter Sakyamuni Buddha
- **Upper floors**: Originally accessible, now closed to visitors due to preservation concerns

### The Town of Yingxian

The pagoda sits in the center of a real Shanxi county town — not a tourist village. Around it you'll find morning markets, noodle shops, chess players, and the kind of unpolished local life that makes a day trip memorable. Walk the streets around the pagoda for 30 minutes before heading back to Datong.

<InfoBox type="tip" title="Pro Tip">
Combine Yingxian with a morning at the Hanging Temple — they're in roughly the same direction from Datong. Leave at 7am, do the Hanging Temple first (less crowded), then continue to Yingxian for lunch and the pagoda. Back in Datong by 4pm.
</InfoBox>

## Why It Matters

The pagoda is proof that nomadic people — the Khitan — built permanent, extraordinary things. It's also a humbling reminder that some of humanity's greatest engineering achievements were built with nothing but wood, skill, and faith. No software, no steel, no nails. Just 3,000 cubic meters of timber and 1,000 years of gravity.
