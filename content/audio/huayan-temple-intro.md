# Huayan Temple: The Greatest Liao Dynasty Temple in China

**Duration:** ~4 minutes
**Location:** Huayan Temple entrance, inside Datong Ancient City
**Order:** Episode 1 of 5

---

## Script

You're standing at the entrance of Huayan Temple — the largest and best-preserved temple complex from the Liao Dynasty anywhere in China. That's not a small claim. The Liao Dynasty ruled northern China from 907 to 1125 AD, and almost nothing they built survives. This temple is the exception.

It's named after the Huayan Sutra — the Avatamsaka Sutra in Sanskrit — one of the most important texts in Mahayana Buddhism. The name means "Flower Garland," and the sutra describes a universe of infinite interconnectedness: every particle contains every other particle, every moment contains every other moment. The temple was designed to embody that idea.

The complex is divided into two sections: the Upper Temple and the Lower Temple. The Upper Temple is grand, ceremonial, built for imperial occasions. The Lower Temple is intimate, scholarly, built for monks to study and meditate. Together they form one of the most complete examples of Liao Dynasty Buddhist architecture in existence.

What makes Huayan different from Yungang? Yungang is carved into the earth — it grew out of a cliff. Huayan is built on the earth — it rises from the ground, timber and tile and bracket sets, a statement of human craft rather than natural canvas. At Yungang, the art is in the subtraction — carving away stone. At Huayan, the art is in the addition — assembling wood, fitting joints, layering paint.

The temple faces east, not south. That's unusual in Chinese architecture, where south-facing is the norm. The reason: the Liao Dynasty was founded by the Khitan people, steppe nomads from what's now Mongolia and Manchuria. In Khitan tradition, the sun — rising in the east — was sacred. Temples faced the sunrise. Buildings faced the sunrise. Even the emperor's throne faced east. This is a Chinese Buddhist temple built by people who were not Chinese, and you can see their culture in the architecture if you know where to look.

You'll spend about an hour here. We'll start with the Upper Temple and its colossal main hall, then move to the Lower Temple with its exquisite Liao Dynasty bookshelves and one of the most beautiful Buddhist statues in China.

## Quick Facts

- **Built:** 1038 AD (Upper) / 1062 AD (Lower), Liao Dynasty
- **Named after:** The Avatamsaka (Flower Garland) Sutra
- **Orientation:** Faces east (Khitan sun worship tradition)
- **Two sections:** Upper Temple (ceremonial) + Lower Temple (scholarly)
- **Time needed:** 1–1.5 hours
