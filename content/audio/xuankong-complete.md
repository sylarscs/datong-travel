# Hanging Temple — Complete Episode Guide (12 Episodes)

## Episode 1: The Canyon — Jinlong Gorge
*3 min · Valley floor*
You're at the bottom of Jinlong Canyon. 75 meters above, a temple clings to the cliff. The geological story: why this canyon exists, how the river carved it, and why Daoists considered it a place where heaven and earth converge. First sight of the temple from the approach road — the moment every photo tries to capture.

## Episode 2: Why Here? — Floods, Silence & Sacred Ground
*3 min · Valley floor*
Three practical reasons to build on a cliff: floods (the river swells 10m in summer), sound (the canyon walls create acoustic isolation for meditation), and sacred geography (Mount Heng looms to the north). Plus the political reason: in 491 AD, the Northern Wei needed a temple that couldn't be attacked.

## Episode 3: The First Glimpse — Approach to the Base
*2 min · Canyon path to temple base*
Walking toward the temple. How it reveals itself gradually — invisible from the approach, then suddenly there. The psychology of the approach: why the builders made you walk toward it rather than see it from a distance.

## Episode 4: At the Base — Looking Up
*2 min · Base of the cliff*
You're directly underneath. The temple looms above, walkways crossing the cliff face at dizzying angles. The vertical posts that look structural but aren't — they sway in the wind. The real support is inside the mountain, where you can't see it.

## Episode 5: The Engineering — How It Hangs
*4 min · Base, before the climb*
The cantilever system: beams driven into the cliff, soaked in tung oil, harder now than when they were cut. Why locust wood. Why this specific cliff face (south-facing, protected from prevailing wind). The seismic secret: the temple moves WITH the mountain during earthquakes — no differential movement, no collapse.

## Episode 6: The Climb Begins — Southern Section
*3 min · Southern walkways*
You're climbing now. The walkways are shoulder-width, the railings low. The Southern Section: smaller halls, a meditation chamber, a sutra library. What daily life was like for the monks who lived here — cramped, vertical, precarious, and completely normal to them.

## Episode 7: The Central Halls — Buddhist Shrines
*4 min · Central section*
The heart of the temple. Main Buddhist shrines with Liao and Ming Dynasty statuary. The central Sakyamuni Buddha, flanked by Ananda and Kasyapa — his two chief disciples. The iconography explained: mudras (hand gestures), asanas (postures), and what they communicate without words.

## Episode 8: The Three Religions Hall — Part 1
*3 min · Upper level*
The only hall in China where Buddha, Laozi, and Confucius share a roof. The statue of Sakyamuni: what makes this depiction different. Buddhism's journey from India through Central Asia to China — and how a foreign religion became Chinese.

## Episode 9: The Three Religions Hall — Part 2
*3 min · Upper level*
Laozi and Confucius: why they're here, why it matters. For most of Chinese history, these three traditions competed for power. A temple that housed all three was a political statement. During the Northern Wei — a dynasty of outsiders ruling a Chinese population — the message was: we contain multitudes.

## Episode 10: The Highest Point — Northern Section
*3 min · Highest walkway, ~90m up*
The view from the top. The river threads through the valley below. Mount Heng on the horizon. The wind is stronger here. The boards creak. This is what the monks saw every morning. Imagine it.

## Episode 11: Details & Decoration — What Most People Miss
*3 min · Throughout the temple*
The carved bracket sets under the eaves — each slightly different, the signature of different craftsmen across different centuries. The worn stone steps, polished smooth by 1,500 years of feet. The small offerings left in corners by visitors who still treat this as sacred. The temple is 40 halls. Each one has a detail worth finding.

## Episode 12: The Descent & What It Means
*2 min · Valley floor, looking back up*
You were up there. On a wooden walkway. Bolted to a cliff. And it held. The Hanging Temple is not a monument to faith — it's a monument to the idea that faith and engineering are the same thing. Both are ways of trusting something you can't see will hold you.
