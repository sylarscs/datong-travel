# Episode 1: The Approach — A Temple in the Sky

**Duration:** ~3 minutes
**Location:** Jinlong Canyon valley floor, looking up at the temple
**Order:** Stop 1 of 4

---

## Script

You're standing at the bottom of Jinlong Canyon. Look up.

Seventy-five meters above you, bolted into a vertical cliff face, is a temple. Not perched on a ledge. Not built into a cave. Bolted. Into the cliff. It's been there for 1,500 years.

Your first reaction is probably the same as everyone's: how? How does a wooden temple — multi-story, multi-hall, with walkways and pavilions — just hang there? How does it survive earthquakes? How does it survive the wind that howls through this canyon? How did anyone even build it in the first place?

The short answer is one of the most elegant engineering solutions in architectural history. You'll learn the details in the next episode. But for now, just look.

The temple is 40 halls and pavilions connected by corridors, bridges, and boardwalks. From here, it looks like a scale model glued to a rock face. But up there, it's a functioning monastery — or it was, for the monks who lived here across fifteen centuries.

The name in Chinese is Xuankong Si — 悬空寺. "Hanging Empty Temple." Not "Hanging Temple" — the "empty" matters. It's a Daoist concept: emptiness is not absence, it's potential. The void is where things can happen.

There's another name, less commonly used: the Temple of the Three Teachings. This is the only temple in China that has housed Buddhism, Daoism, and Confucianism simultaneously, under one roof, for its entire history. Three religions that spent much of Chinese history competing for imperial favor, sharing a hallway. You'll see their statues together in the highest hall.

Why build here? Three reasons, all of them practical.

First, floods. The river below, the Hunhe, swells dramatically during the summer rainy season. Building on the cliff means the temple never floods.

Second, sound. The canyon walls block wind noise. Up in the temple, it's almost silent — perfect for meditation.

Third, according to Daoist belief, this canyon is a place where the energy of heaven and earth converge. Building a temple here means building at a point of maximum spiritual power.

Practical and sacred. Engineering and faith. That tension runs through everything about this place.

Take a few more minutes here at the bottom. The light is best in the morning, when the sun hits the cliff face. Walk around. Find your angle. Then head up — the entrance is on the left side of the canyon.

---

## Quick Facts

- **Height:** 75 meters above the valley floor
- **Built:** 491 AD, Northern Wei Dynasty
- **Halls & pavilions:** 40
- **Religions:** Buddhism, Daoism, Confucianism (all three)
- **Best photo spot:** Valley floor, looking up, morning light
