# Yungang Grottoes — Complete Cave Reference

A comprehensive reference covering all 45 major caves, organized by section. Designed to be adapted into the full audio guide experience. Caves marked ★★★ are covered by dedicated episodes.

---

## Section 1: Eastern Caves (1–4)

*Walking time: ~20 minutes. These are the first caves you encounter, built later in Yungang's history (post-494 AD). Smaller scale, but worth a brief look to understand how the site evolved.*

### Cave 1 & 2 — Twin Caves ★
**10 min combined**

Caves 1 and 2 are a matched pair — identical layout, mirror-image design. Both feature a central square pillar (stupa) carved on all four sides, with Buddha niches on each face. The outer walls show scenes from the Jataka tales — stories of the Buddha's previous lives.

What's notable here isn't the scale (these are modest) but the structure. The central-pillar design came from Indian cave temples and was adapted by Central Asian Buddhists before reaching China. These two caves are among the clearest examples of that architectural lineage at Yungang.

The carvings are weathered — these caves are more exposed to wind than the central section — but the overall composition is still clear. Look for the flying apsaras on the ceiling of Cave 1, some of the best-preserved in the eastern section.

### Cave 3 — The Unfinished Giant ★
**5 min**

Cave 3 is the largest single cave chamber at Yungang — so large the carvers never finished it. The chamber is 25 meters wide and 15 meters high, with three colossal Buddha figures in progress on the back wall. You can see the work at different stages: the central Buddha is nearly complete, the two flanking Bodhisattvas are roughed out but not detailed.

This is the only place at Yungang where you can see the carving process frozen mid-task. Look at the unfinished figures' faces — the rough stone blocks that would have become features, the chisel marks still visible. It's the closest thing to watching 5th-century sculptors at work.

Scholars believe Cave 3 was meant to be the crowning achievement of the later period, but the Northern Wei moved their capital to Luoyang in 494 AD, and royal funding stopped. The cave was simply abandoned.

### Cave 4 — The Weathered One
**3 min**

Cave 4 is the most eroded in the eastern section — the front wall is almost entirely gone, exposing the interior. A single standing Buddha remains, about 6 meters tall, his features softened by wind and rain into something almost abstract. Brief stop only.

<InfoBox type="tip" title="Eastern Section Verdict">
If you're on a tight schedule, walk through Caves 1–2 quickly (5 min each), pause at Cave 3 (the unfinished giant is worth seeing), and skip Cave 4. Dedicate your time to the central sections instead.
</InfoBox>

## Section 2: Central-East — The Imperial Showcase (5–13)

*Walking time: 45–60 minutes. This is the heart of Yungang. If you only have an hour, spend it here.*

### Caves 5 & 6 — The Twin Caves ★★★
**15–20 min** — *Dedicated episode available*

### Caves 7 & 8 — The Hindu Connection ★★
**10 min combined**

Another matched pair, these twin caves are famous for something unique at Yungang: Hindu deities carved alongside Buddhist figures. On the entrance wall of Cave 7, you'll find Shiva (the destroyer) and Vishnu (the preserver) — major Hindu gods — integrated into a Buddhist cave complex.

This isn't religious confusion. It's evidence of how Buddhism traveled: as it moved from India through Central Asia, it absorbed local deities along the way. Hindu gods became Buddhist protector figures. The process is called syncretism, and Caves 7–8 are among the clearest examples in Chinese cave art.

The painted ceilings here are also exceptional. Unlike most caves where original colors have faded, Caves 7–8 retain visible pigments — deep blues, reds, traces of gold. The best-preserved painted ceiling at Yungang is in Cave 7. Look up.

### Caves 9 & 10 — The Five Flowers Caves ★★
**10 min combined**

Locals call these the "Five Flowers Caves" because of the elaborate architectural decoration carved into every surface. The fronts of both caves are designed to look like wooden palace halls — carved columns, bracket sets, roof beams, even tile patterns — all executed in stone. It's architecture pretending to be architecture, and it's extraordinarily detailed.

Cave 9 features a large seated Buddha with an elaborate canopy overhead, surrounded by thousands of tiny seated Buddhas in neat rows. Cave 10 has a similar layout, with the added feature of carved windows — stone frames carved to look like wooden lattice screens — on either side of the main niche.

These caves date to the peak of Yungang's royal patronage period (480s AD) and it shows. No expense was spared. The craftsmen were showing off.

### Cave 11 — The Square Pillar Cave ★
**5 min**

Cave 11 centers on a square stupa pillar with a standing Buddha in a niche on each face. The walls are covered with small donor niches — individual sponsors paid for single small Buddha figures and had their names carved beneath. This is the 5th-century equivalent of a donor wall at a modern museum. Look for the Chinese characters carved below figures on the lower registers; these are names of people who funded this cave.

### Cave 12 — The Music Cave ★★
**10 min** — *Dedicated episode available*

### Cave 13 — The Cross-Ankled Bodhisattva ★
**5 min**

Cave 13 holds a massive cross-ankled Bodhisattva — nearly 13 meters tall, sitting in a posture that's unusual for Yungang. The cross-ankled pose (legs bent, ankles crossed) originated in Central Asian Buddhist art and represents royal ease: the figure is both relaxed and alert.

Look at the Bodhisattva's right arm: it's supported by a small standing figure carved into the wall. This "arm-support figure" is unique at Yungang. The small figure is a lokapala — a Buddhist guardian deity — pressed into service as a literal structural support for a statue that weighs several tons. Practical engineering, sacred symbolism, all in one gesture.

## Section 3: Central — The Tan Yao Caves (14–20)

*Walking time: 30–40 minutes. The original five caves that started Yungang, plus two smaller ones.*

### Cave 14 — Small & Eroded
**2 min**

Cave 14 is badly damaged — the front wall is gone, the interior exposed, and what remains is heavily eroded. A seated Buddha is still recognizable but his features are nearly erased. Skip unless you're being thorough.

### Cave 15 — The Ten Thousand Buddhas Cave ★★
**5–8 min**

Cave 15 is small but unforgettable. Every wall surface — every centimeter of every wall — is covered with tiny, identical seated Buddha figures in neat rows. Thousands of them. The effect is hypnotic: a wallpaper of enlightenment, the same face repeated into infinity.

This is the "Ten Thousand Buddhas" (万佛) motif at its most extreme. The number isn't literal — there aren't exactly 10,000 — but the idea is that the Buddha's presence fills every direction, every dimension, every scale. Walk in, turn around slowly, and see if you can find a single square centimeter that doesn't have a Buddha on it.

## Section 4: Western Caves (21–45)

*Walking time: 20–30 minutes. These are the latest caves at Yungang, carved after the imperial court moved to Luoyang in 494 AD. Smaller, less elaborate, but interesting for what they reveal about the site's later life.*

### Caves 21–29 — The Post-Imperial Period ★
**10–15 min for the group**

After 494 AD, imperial funding stopped. But carving didn't. These caves were sponsored by local officials, merchants, and Buddhist societies rather than emperors. The scale is dramatically smaller — most niches hold figures under 2 meters — but the variety is greater. Different styles, different patrons, different visions.

Cave 24 is the standout in this group: a well-preserved seated Buddha with two standing attendants, showing the mature late-Northern Wei style. The facial features are more Chinese, less Central Asian — the foreign influence that dominated the Tan Yao caves has been absorbed and naturalized over two generations.

Cave 28 is notable for its painted ceiling, which retains more original color than almost any other cave in the western section.

### Caves 30–37 — The Folk Period ★
**10 min for the group**

These caves represent the very end of Yungang's active period — the 520s AD and later. The carvings are rougher, the scale smaller, the patrons humbler. Many are essentially family shrines: a single niche with a single Buddha, commissioned by a single household.

Cave 33 has an interesting inscription: a dated dedication from 523 AD, one of the latest at Yungang. By this point, the Northern Wei Dynasty was unraveling — it would collapse completely within a decade — and you can sense the uncertainty in the modest scale and hasty execution.

Cave 37 features a carved stupa that's unusually slender and elegant — a late-period experiment in form that the earlier carvers, bound by imperial specifications, wouldn't have attempted.

### Caves 38–45 — The End ★
**5–10 min for the group**

The last caves at the far western end. Many are locked or too small to enter. Cave 39 is the largest in this group, with a central pillar stupa that echoes the layout of Caves 1–2 — a full-circle return to the oldest design at the site, 60 years and 50,000 statues later.

Cave 45, the very last numbered cave, is barely a cave at all — more of a niche, holding a single small Buddha, badly eroded. It's a quiet ending. No grand finale. Just the cliff running out of space, the patrons running out of money, the dynasty running out of time.

<InfoBox type="highlight" title="Western Section Verdict">
If you're short on time, skip the western section. It's for completists and return visitors. The central caves (5–20) contain everything that makes Yungang a UNESCO site. But if you have a full morning and want to feel the full arc of the site — from imperial ambition through local piety to quiet decline — walk the full kilometer from east to west.
</InfoBox>
