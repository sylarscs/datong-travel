# Welcome to Yungang: Introduction

**Duration:** ~3 minutes
**Location:** Entrance plaza, before entering the cave area
**Order:** Stop 1 of 6

---

## Script

Hi, and welcome to the Yungang Grottoes. My name's [name], and I'm going to walk you through one of the most extraordinary places on Earth — a kilometer-long sandstone cliff carved with 51,000 Buddha statues.

Before we go in, let me set the scene. It's the year 460 AD. The Roman Empire has just collapsed in the West. In China, the Northern Wei Dynasty rules from a city called Pingcheng — what we now call Datong. The emperor has just converted to Buddhism, and he wants to make a statement. Not with words. With stone.

He orders the carving of five colossal Buddhas into this cliff face — each one said to bear his own likeness, and those of his four predecessors. The message was clear: the emperor is not just a ruler. He is a living Buddha.

What started as an imperial PR project grew into something much bigger. Over the next 60 years, hundreds of craftsmen — Indian, Persian, Central Asian, Chinese — carved an entire spiritual world into this cliff. By the time they stopped, there were 252 caves and over 51,000 statues here, ranging from a 17-meter colossus to figures small enough to fit in the palm of your hand.

UNESCO calls Yungang "a masterpiece of early Chinese Buddhist cave art." What makes it special — and different from the Mogao Caves in Dunhuang or the Longmen Grottoes near Luoyang — is that you can see the Silk Road happening in the stone. Indian gods dance next to Chinese dragons. Persian lions guard Buddhist thrones. Greek-style columns frame Central Asian faces. This is what globalization looked like in the 5th century.

Over the next hour, I'll take you through six stops — the essential caves that tell the story of Yungang. You'll see the largest Buddha here, the most detailed carvings, the musicians' cave, and the iconic open-air Buddha that's become the symbol of Datong itself.

A few practical things before we start. No flash photography inside the caves — the pigments are 1,500 years old and extremely fragile. Stay on the paved paths. And if you need to use the restroom, there's one near the entrance and one by Cave 20 at the far end. There's nothing in between, so plan accordingly.

Ready? Let's go to our first stop — Caves 5 and 6, the heart of Yungang.

---

## Quick Facts

- **Total caves:** 252 (45 major caves open to public)
- **Total statues:** 51,000+
- **Carving period:** 460–524 AD (Northern Wei Dynasty)
- **UNESCO World Heritage:** Since 2001
- **Length of cliff:** ~1 kilometer
- **Typical visit:** 3–4 hours for a thorough tour
