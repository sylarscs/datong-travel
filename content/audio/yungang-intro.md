# Welcome to Yungang: Introduction

**Duration:** ~3 minutes
**Location:** Entrance plaza, before entering the cave area
**Order:** Stop 1 of 6

---

## Script

You're standing at the entrance to one of the most extraordinary places on Earth — a kilometer-long sandstone cliff carved with 51,000 Buddha statues.

It's the year 460 AD. The Western Roman Empire is collapsing. In China, the Northern Wei Dynasty rules from a city called Pingcheng — what we now call Datong. A new emperor has just converted to Buddhism, and he wants to make a statement. Not with words. With stone.

He orders the carving of five colossal Buddhas into this very cliff — each one said to bear his own likeness, and those of his four predecessors. The message: the emperor is not just a ruler. He is a living Buddha.

What started as imperial PR grew into something far bigger. Over the next 60 years, hundreds of craftsmen — Indian, Persian, Central Asian, Chinese — carved an entire spiritual world into this cliff. By the time they stopped, there were 252 caves holding over 51,000 statues, from a 17-meter colossus to figures small enough to fit in your palm.

UNESCO calls Yungang "a masterpiece of early Chinese Buddhist cave art." What makes it different from the Mogao Caves in Dunhuang or the Longmen Grottoes near Luoyang is that you can see the Silk Road happening in the stone. Indian gods dance next to Chinese dragons. Persian lions guard Buddhist thrones. Greek-style columns frame Central Asian faces. This is what globalization looked like in the 5th century.

Over the next hour, this guide takes you through six essential stops. You'll see the largest Buddha here, the most detailed carvings, the musicians' cave, and the iconic open-air Buddha that's become the symbol of Datong itself.

A few practical notes. No flash photography inside the caves — the pigments are 1,500 years old and extremely fragile. Stay on the paved paths. Restrooms are at the entrance and near Cave 20 at the far end — nothing in between.

Ready? First stop: Caves 5 and 6, the heart of Yungang.

---

## Quick Facts

- **Total caves:** 252 (45 major caves open to public)
- **Total statues:** 51,000+
- **Carving period:** 460–524 AD (Northern Wei Dynasty)
- **UNESCO World Heritage:** Since 2001
- **Length of cliff:** ~1 kilometer
- **Typical visit:** 3–4 hours for a thorough tour
