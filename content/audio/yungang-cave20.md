# Cave 20: The Open-Air Buddha

**Duration:** ~4 minutes
**Location:** Western end of the main cliff, Yungang Grottoes
**Order:** Stop 3 of 6 in the audio guide series

---

## Script

Welcome to Cave 20. This is the one you've seen in every photo of Yungang — the giant Buddha sitting in the open air, exposed to the sky, with nothing between him and you but 1,500 years of history.

Let me tell you why this cave looks different from the others, because it's the first thing you'll notice. There's no front wall. It collapsed centuries ago, probably during an earthquake. And here's the thing: that accident created the most photographed spot in the entire grotto complex. You're standing in front of the Yungang Grottoes' postcard shot.

The Buddha himself is 14 meters tall — that's about the height of a four-story building. He's seated in the traditional meditation posture: legs crossed, hands resting in his lap, eyes half-closed. What I want you to notice is his expression. It's not stern. It's not ecstatic. It's what the Buddhists call "the half-closed eyes of compassion" — he's looking inward and outward at the same time. Approachable. Calm. After 1,500 years of wind and rain and earthquakes, he's still here, still calm.

This Buddha was carved around 460 AD. To put that in perspective: this was before the fall of the Western Roman Empire. Before the Vikings. Before Islam existed. When this statue was carved, King Arthur — if he existed at all — was a minor warlord in post-Roman Britain. That's how old this is.

The cave is one of the "Five Caves of Tan Yao," named after the monk who convinced the emperor to build them. And here's where it gets interesting from a human perspective. The five Buddhas in caves 16 through 20 are said to represent five Northern Wei emperors. The idea was: the emperor is a living Buddha, and carving his likeness into a sacred cliff makes his rule divinely sanctioned. It was political propaganda, carved in stone.

Look at the halo behind the Buddha's head. See those tiny figures carved into it? Those are flying celestials — apsaras — Hindu spirits that Buddhism adopted as it traveled from India through Central Asia to China. This one statue contains three civilizations: Indian religion, Central Asian artistic technique, Chinese imperial politics. The Silk Road, frozen in sandstone.

Now, here's what I want you to do. Walk a few steps to your right. See the smaller standing Buddha? That was originally inside the cave too, but when the front wall collapsed, he became exposed. Notice his hand gesture — right hand raised, palm out. That's the "fear not" mudra. It says: don't be afraid. You're welcome here.

One more thing before you move on. The surface of the Buddha is pockmarked — you can see small holes all over his chest and face. Those aren't damage. They're from a Qing Dynasty restoration, when workers drilled holes and inserted wooden pegs to attach a layer of clay plaster and paint. The plaster has long since fallen off, but the holes remain. It's a reminder that this statue has been maintained, repaired, and cared for across fifteen centuries.

Give yourself a minute. Take the photo. Then we'll walk to Cave 19, which holds the second-largest Buddha at Yungang — and a very different version of the same idea.

## Quick Facts

- **Buddha height:** 14 meters
- **Carved:** ~460 AD, Northern Wei Dynasty
- **Part of:** The Five Caves of Tan Yao (Caves 16–20)
- **Style:** Central Asian-influenced early Buddhist art
- **Best photo time:** Morning, 9–11 AM, when sunlight hits the face directly
