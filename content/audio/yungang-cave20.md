# Cave 20: The Open-Air Buddha

**Duration:** ~4 minutes
**Location:** Western end of the main cliff, Yungang Grottoes
**Order:** Stop 3 of 6

---

## Script

This is the one. The photo you've seen a hundred times. The giant Buddha sitting in the open air, exposed to the sky, with nothing between him and you but 1,500 years of wind and rain.

First, notice what's different: there's no front wall. It collapsed centuries ago, probably during an earthquake. That accident — a structural failure — created the most photographed spot in the entire grotto complex. You're looking at the Yungang Grottoes' postcard.

The Buddha is 14 meters tall — roughly four stories. He sits in traditional meditation posture: legs crossed, hands resting in his lap, eyes half-closed. Focus on his expression. It's not stern. It's not ecstatic. It's what Buddhists call "the half-closed eyes of compassion" — looking inward and outward at the same time. After 1,500 years of earthquakes and storms, he's still here, still calm.

This Buddha was carved around 460 AD. To put that in perspective: the Western Roman Empire hadn't fully collapsed yet. The Vikings didn't exist. Islam didn't exist. When this statue was carved, King Arthur — if he existed at all — was a minor warlord in post-Roman Britain.

The cave belongs to the "Five Caves of Tan Yao," named after the monk who convinced the emperor to build them. And here's where the story gets human. The five colossal Buddhas in Caves 16 through 20 are said to represent five Northern Wei emperors. The idea was simple: the emperor is a living Buddha. Carve his likeness into a sacred cliff, and his rule becomes divinely sanctioned. It was political propaganda, executed in stone.

Look at the halo behind the Buddha's head. The tiny figures carved into it are flying celestials — apsaras — Hindu spirits that Buddhism absorbed as it traveled from India through Central Asia to China. This single statue contains three civilizations: Indian religion, Central Asian artistic technique, Chinese imperial politics. The Silk Road, frozen in sandstone.

Now walk a few steps to the right. The smaller standing Buddha was originally inside the cave too, but the collapsing wall exposed him. Notice his right hand — raised, palm out. That's the abhaya mudra, the "fear not" gesture. Don't be afraid. You're welcome here.

One last detail. The Buddha's surface is pockmarked with small holes across his chest and face. Those aren't damage. During the Qing Dynasty, workers drilled those holes and inserted wooden pegs to attach a layer of clay plaster and paint. The plaster has long since fallen off, but the holes remain — evidence that this statue has been maintained and cared for across fifteen centuries.

Take a minute. Take the photo. The next stop is Cave 19, with the second-largest Buddha at Yungang — and a very different version of the same idea.

## Quick Facts

- **Buddha height:** 14 meters
- **Carved:** ~460 AD, Northern Wei Dynasty
- **Part of:** The Five Caves of Tan Yao (Caves 16–20)
- **Style:** Central Asian-influenced early Buddhist art
- **Best photo time:** Morning, 9–11 AM, when sunlight hits the face directly
