# The Upper Temple: China's Largest Buddhist Hall

**Duration:** ~3 minutes
**Location:** Upper Temple, main hall
**Order:** Episode 2 of 5

---

## Script

You're standing in front of the Mahavira Hall — the main hall of Huayan Temple's Upper Section. Take a moment to absorb the scale. This is one of the largest Buddhist halls in China: 53 meters wide, 27 meters deep, sitting on a 4-meter-high platform. It doesn't just dominate the temple complex. It dominates the neighborhood.

The hall was rebuilt in 1140 AD after a fire destroyed the original Liao Dynasty structure. The Jin Dynasty, which had conquered the Liao, rebuilt it bigger. Conquest, in medieval China, often meant outdoing the previous regime's architecture. If you can't beat their art, build something even larger.

Step inside. Let your eyes adjust to the dimness — the hall is lit only by natural light filtering through the lattice windows. The first thing you'll notice is the five colossal Buddha statues on the central altar. These represent the Five Wisdom Buddhas of Vajrayana Buddhism: Vairocana (center, white, the cosmic Buddha), Akshobhya (east, blue), Ratnasambhava (south, yellow), Amitabha (west, red), and Amoghasiddhi (north, green).

They're Ming Dynasty restorations — the originals were Liao — but the iconography is correct to the original conception. Notice the mudras, the hand gestures. Each Buddha makes a different one, corresponding to their wisdom: teaching, meditation, fearlessness, giving, and the earth-touching gesture of enlightenment.

Now look at the walls. Every surface is covered with Ming Dynasty frescoes — Buddhist deities, guardian figures, celestial musicians, scenes from sutras, all rendered in mineral pigments that have barely faded in 500 years. The total painted area is over 880 square meters. That's larger than most apartments.

The ceiling is a masterpiece of Ming woodwork: intricate bracket sets, painted beams, coffered panels decorated with dragons and lotuses. Look up. This is what imperial craftsmanship looks like — every joint hidden, every surface decorated, no detail too small to matter.

Behind the main altar, there's a smaller shrine with a standing Guanyin — the Bodhisattva of Compassion — with a thousand arms radiating from her body. Each hand holds a different tool for saving beings from suffering. It's a powerful image, and the contrast with the calm, seated Buddhas in front is intentional: the Buddha is at rest, the Bodhisattva is at work.

## Quick Facts

- **Hall:** 53m wide × 27m deep, on 4m platform
- **Rebuilt:** 1140 AD (Jin Dynasty, after fire)
- **Five Buddhas:** Ming Dynasty restorations of Liao originals
- **Frescoes:** 880+ sq meters, Ming Dynasty, mineral pigments
- **Ceiling:** Coffered Ming woodwork with dragons and lotuses
