# The Pagoda & Gardens: Huayan's Quiet Side

**Duration:** ~2 minutes
**Location:** Huayan Pagoda and surrounding gardens
**Order:** Episode 4 of 5

---

## Script

Behind the main halls, the Huayan Pagoda rises five stories — about 43 meters — above the temple complex. It's a modern reconstruction, built in 2010 to replace a Liao Dynasty original that collapsed in the 19th century. Before you dismiss it as "not old," consider: the Liao Dynasty built wooden pagodas that lasted 800 years before they fell. The new one uses the same joinery techniques, the same timber species, the same bracket sets. If it lasts 800 years, it will have earned its place.

The pagoda is a square-based wooden structure in the Liao style — broader and more solid than the slender Tang Dynasty pagodas further south. Each level has a balcony; you can climb to the top for a view over the temple roofs and the ancient city. The staircases are narrow and steep — Liao architecture didn't prioritize visitor comfort — but the view from the top is worth the climb. Looking down, you can see the temple's layout as the architects intended: the main halls aligned east to west, the subsidiary buildings flanking them, the whole complex a geometric diagram of the Buddhist cosmos.

The gardens surrounding the pagoda are planted with pines and scholarly rocks — the traditional elements of a Chinese temple garden. In spring, the apricot trees bloom white. In autumn, the ginkgo leaves turn gold. If you're here at the right season, spend ten minutes just sitting here. After the intensity of Yungang and the vertigo of the Hanging Temple, Huayan's gardens are a place to process what you've seen.

## Quick Facts

- **Pagoda:** 43m, 5 stories, rebuilt 2010 in original Liao style
- **Original:** Collapsed 19th century, was ~800 years old
- **Climb:** Narrow stairs, view over temple roofs and city
- **Gardens:** Pine, apricot, ginkgo — best in spring and autumn
