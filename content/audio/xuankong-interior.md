# Episode 3: Inside the Temple — Three Religions, One Roof

**Duration:** ~4 minutes
**Location:** Inside the temple walkways
**Order:** Stop 3 of 4

---

## Script

You're inside now. The walkways are narrow — about shoulder-width — and the railings are low. If you're afraid of heights, this might be the moment to focus on the architecture and not look down. You're 75 meters above the canyon floor, on a wooden boardwalk held up by beams driven into a cliff. It's unsettling. It's supposed to be. A certain amount of fear has always been part of the pilgrimage.

The temple is organized vertically. You'll climb as you go — the Southern Section first, then higher to the Northern Section. About 60 walkways and staircases connect the 40 halls. The route is one-way, so you can't get lost. Just keep going up.

The halls are small — closet-sized, some of them. This isn't a grand cathedral. It's a cluster of intimate chambers carved into and extending from the cliff. Each one has a specific purpose. A meditation room here. A sutra library there. A shrine. A monk's cell. Life here was cramped, vertical, precarious — and completely normal to the people who lived it.

The highlight is at the top: the San Jiao Hall, the Hall of Three Teachings. This is the only place in China where you'll find statues of Sakyamuni — the historical Buddha — sitting next to Laozi, the founder of Daoism, sitting next to Confucius, the philosopher whose ethics shaped Chinese civilization for two millennia.

They're not fighting. They're not competing. They're just... there. Together. Equal.

This is radical. For most of Chinese history, these three traditions jockeyed for imperial favor, sometimes violently. Buddhist monasteries were burned in Daoist purges. Daoist temples were converted to government offices by Confucian officials. The idea that all three could coexist — that they were three paths to the same truth, rather than competing truths — was a minority view, and a brave one.

The Northern Wei was a regime of outsiders — steppe nomads who had conquered northern China and were trying to figure out how to rule a civilization far older than their own. Building a temple where all three belief systems shared a roof was a political statement: we don't pick sides. We contain multitudes.

As you walk back down, notice the details. The carved brackets under the eaves, each one a slightly different pattern — the work of different craftsmen across different centuries. The worn stone steps, polished smooth by a thousand years of sandals and shoes. The small offerings left in corners — a coin, a strand of incense, a note — by visitors who still treat this place as sacred.

The temple wasn't built to impress. It was built to work. And for 1,500 years, it has.

## Quick Facts

- **Halls:** 40, connected by 60+ walkways and staircases
- **Route:** One-way, south to north, bottom to top
- **Hall of Three Teachings:** Buddha, Laozi, Confucius together
- **Climb duration:** 30–45 minutes at a normal pace
- **Notable:** Low railings, narrow passages — not for severe vertigo
