# The Lower Temple: The Library That Survived a Thousand Years

**Duration:** ~4 minutes
**Location:** Lower Temple, Bhagavan Sutra Hall
**Order:** Episode 3 of 5

---

## Script

Walk south from the Upper Temple — about 100 meters — and you'll enter the Lower Temple. The atmosphere changes immediately. The Upper Temple is grand and public. This is quiet and intimate. You're entering a space built for monks, not emperors.

The heart of the Lower Temple is the Bhagavan Sutra Hall — and inside it are the oldest and most beautiful bookshelves you will ever see.

These are the "Heavenly Palace Cabinets" — 38 wooden cabinets arranged around the perimeter of the hall, built in 1038 AD during the Liao Dynasty. They're made of nanmu, a fine-grained hardwood that resists insects and decay, and they've survived nearly a thousand years without significant restoration. The cabinets are connected by covered walkways and staircases — a miniature architectural complex inside a building. The design is called tiangong louge, "heavenly palace pavilions," and this is the finest surviving example.

The cabinets were built to house the Tripitaka — the complete Buddhist canon, thousands of sutras, each volume wrapped in silk and stored in its own compartment. Monks would climb the narrow stairs between the cabinets to retrieve texts for study. The entire hall was a functioning library, designed for the preservation and transmission of knowledge.

Look at the joinery. No nails. No glue. Every connection is a precisely fitted wood joint — mortise and tenon, dovetail, lap joint — the same techniques used in the Hanging Temple. These are the same Liao Dynasty carpenters, or their direct students, working in the same tradition. The cabinets are furniture, but they're also architecture, and they're also sculpture. Categories break down when the craftsmanship is this good.

In the center of the hall sits a breathtaking statue of Sakyamuni — the historical Buddha — flanked by two standing Bodhisattvas. This is widely considered one of the finest Buddhist sculptures in China. The Buddha's face is serene but not distant; there's a warmth to the expression that's unusual in Chinese Buddhist art, which often favors formal impassivity. The flowing lines of the robes, the delicate fingers, the slight asymmetry of the posture that makes the figure feel alive rather than rigid — this is Liao Dynasty sculpture at its absolute peak.

The hall is dim, lit by small windows and the glow of butter lamps. The wood has darkened with age to a deep honey-brown, almost black in the corners. The air smells of old timber and incense. If you came here at the right moment — early morning, no other visitors — you'd understand why monks chose this place to spend their lives reading.

## Quick Facts

- **Bhagavan Sutra Hall:** Built 1038 AD, Liao Dynasty
- **Cabinets:** 38 nanmu wood cabinets, original Liao Dynasty
- **Style:** Tiangong louge ("heavenly palace pavilions")
- **Purpose:** Housed the complete Buddhist canon (Tripitaka)
- **Center statue:** Sakyamuni Buddha + 2 Bodhisattvas, Liao Dynasty originals
