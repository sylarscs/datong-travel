# Caves 16–19: The Emperors' Caves

**Duration:** ~5 minutes
**Location:** Central section of the main cliff
**Order:** Stop 4 of 6

---

## Script

These four caves — 16, 17, 18, and 19 — are the originals. The first ones carved. The reason Yungang exists at all.

Together with Cave 20, which you've just seen, they're known as the Five Caves of Tan Yao. Tan Yao was a monk — a very smart, very persuasive monk — who saw an opportunity. The year is 460 AD. Emperor Wencheng has just taken the throne. His predecessor, Emperor Taiwu, had been a Daoist who violently suppressed Buddhism: temples destroyed, monks executed, scriptures burned. The Buddhist community is traumatized and depleted.

Tan Yao goes to Wencheng with a proposal. Carve five colossal Buddhas into the cliff at the capital. Make each one a portrait — not a literal portrait, but a symbolic one — of an emperor. Wencheng himself, plus his four predecessors. Including, pointedly, the anti-Buddhist Taiwu.

The message was brilliant. By carving Taiwu as a Buddha, Tan Yao wasn't condemning him. He was redeeming him. Buddhism doesn't seek revenge; it seeks compassion. Even the man who tried to destroy the faith can become a Buddha. And for Wencheng, the political logic was irresistible: if your predecessors are all Buddhas, then you, too, are divine.

This is the moment Yungang was born. Politics and religion, welded together in stone.

Cave 19 is the largest of the five — a 16.8-meter seated Buddha, slightly smaller than Cave 5's colossus but older. Walk inside and look at his face. It's rounder, softer, more abstract than the statues in the later caves. This is early Northern Wei style, heavily influenced by the giant Buddhas of Bamiyan in Afghanistan — figures that the Taliban destroyed in 2001. Yungang is now the best-preserved example of this Central Asian tradition.

Cave 18 is the most dramatic. The main Buddha stands — not sits — 15.5 meters tall. His robe is covered with tiny relief Buddhas, hundreds of them carved into the fabric itself. Art historians call this the "Buddha of a Thousand Buddhas" effect. The standing posture, the elaborate robe, the sheer verticality of the figure — it commands attention in a way the seated Buddhas don't.

Cave 17 holds a seated Bodhisattva — a Buddha-to-be — rather than a fully enlightened Buddha. This is unusual and may represent an emperor who was still alive when the cave was carved. In Buddhist iconography, a Bodhisattva is someone who has achieved enlightenment but chooses to remain in the world to help others. The parallel with a living emperor is clear.

Cave 16 is the smallest of the five, with a standing Buddha whose face shows the most individual character. Some scholars believe this one is the closest to an actual portrait of Emperor Wencheng himself.

Here's what to notice as you walk between these four caves. Each Buddha is slightly different — different posture, different facial structure, different drapery. The same basic idea — emperor as deity — expressed four different ways. It's a reminder that even under an imperial mandate, individual sculptors had room to interpret, to improvise, to bring their own vision to the cliff.

## Quick Facts

- **Carved:** 460–470 AD (the oldest caves at Yungang)
- **Commissioned by:** Monk Tan Yao, for Emperor Wencheng
- **Represent:** Five Northern Wei emperors as living Buddhas
- **Style:** Early Northern Wei, heavily Central Asian-influenced
- **Key difference:** Each Buddha has a distinct face and posture
