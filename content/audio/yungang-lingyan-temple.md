# Lingyan Temple: The Gateway to the Grottoes

**Duration:** ~3 minutes
**Location:** Lingyan Temple, inside Yungang entrance plaza
**Order:** Episode 1 of 15

---

## Script

Before you reach the caves, there's a temple. Most visitors walk through it without stopping, eyes fixed on the cliff ahead. That's a mistake.

This is Lingyan Temple — the "Temple of the Divine Cliff." It's a modern reconstruction, built in the 1990s on the site of a Northern Wei original that was destroyed centuries ago. The old temple was where pilgrims prepared themselves before entering the sacred cave complex. The new one serves the same purpose, whether you realize it or not.

The temple is built in the Northern Wei style: heavy timber framing, sweeping eaves, bracket sets that flare outward like wings. Look at the roof — that distinctive curve at the eaves isn't just decorative. It's a cantilever, distributing the roof's weight outward so the walls don't have to bear it. The same engineering principle you'll see at the Hanging Temple, 65 kilometers away. Northern Wei builders knew their structural dynamics.

Inside the main hall, there's a modern Buddha statue — not ancient, not a relic — but the incense is real, and so are the worshippers. This is still an active temple, not a museum piece. You may see monks chanting, or locals lighting incense and bowing. Be respectful. Don't photograph people without permission.

The temple also houses a small museum on the ground floor — worth 10 minutes if you want to see artifacts from the Northern Wei period: pottery, bronze mirrors, fragments of statuary from the caves. The English signage is decent.

But the real reason to pause here is psychological. The temple is the threshold. Behind it, the cliff. Inside the cliff, 1,500 years of Buddhist art. Take a breath. The caves aren't going anywhere.

- **Built:** 1990s reconstruction of Northern Wei original

- **Active temple:** Yes — worshippers may be present
